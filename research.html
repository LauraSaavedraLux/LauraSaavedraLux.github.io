My research sits at the intersection of political economy, conflict, and development. I am interested in how armed conflict shapes the economic and political trajectory of societies, particularly at a localised level. I have examined these questions in countries affected by organised violence across regional contexts, including Peru, Mozambique, and Iraq. Methodologically, I integrate different methods and forms of evidence, from conducting fieldwork and working with remote sensing and administrative data to implementing causal inference strategies and spatial econometrics.

Post-conflict recovery & state-building
Quid pro quo: wartime economies, civilian support, and the persistence of armed violence — under review, American Journal of Political Science
Solo-authored  ·  UNU-WIDER working paper ↗
Why do some conflict-affected areas remain arenas of violent contestation while others transition to peace? I argue that economic networks developed during intrastate conflict — wartime economies — give rise to continued instability by shaping civilian allegiances toward armed actors and away from the state. Using spatial instrumental variable regressions on data from Peru's internal armed conflict, I show that districts with stronger wartime economies experienced significantly more post-conflict violence.

Using evidence from Islamic State occupation in Iraq, we examine how the duration and nature of rebel rule affects post-occupation development trajectories and civilian attitudes toward state and non-state institutions.

Resources, investment & conflict
Blood and capital: foreign direct investment and civilian targeting in African conflict zones — under review, Comparative Political Studies
With Lorenzo Crippa  ·  UNU-WIDER working paper ↗
We analyse how inward FDI received amid ongoing violence shapes patterns of civilian harm. Drawing on geocoded FDI and conflict event data across sub-Saharan Africa, we find that immobile extractive and industrial investment significantly increases civilian casualties within proximity of investment sites — with implications for responsible investment policy in fragile settings.

Current projects
Engines of economic growth in fragile and conflict-affected settings
IFPRI  ·  UNU-WIDER  ·  World Bank  ·  Ongoing

Why do some communities maintain economic dynamism despite conflict, climate shocks, and political instability — while others stagnate? This research programme examines three mutually reinforcing engines of local economic growth in fragile settings: productive livelihoods and market linkages, human capital, and trust and collective action. A central argument is that these engines do not simply weaken under fragility and recover after it — they transform. The programme combines diagnostic analysis, comparative country research in sub-Saharan Africa, and collaboration with implementing partners to generate rigorous, policy-relevant evidence on what works.
